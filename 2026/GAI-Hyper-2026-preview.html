Aim and Scope

Generative AI (GAI) has seen rapid advancements and gained immense popularity in recent years, transforming how intelligent systems generate, reason, interact, and act across digital and physical environments. Its evolution from content generation toward autonomous, interactive, and collaborative intelligence can be viewed through the XYZ perspectives of xLM, yAI, and zTech. xLM encompasses a rapidly expanding family of large and foundation models, including Large Language Models (LLMs), Vision-Language Models (VLMs), Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), and other emerging model architectures. Building upon xLM, yAI marks a shift from generative capability to emerging forms of intelligence that can reason, create, plan, act, adapt, and collaborate. These advances are further giving rise to zTech—a new wave of intelligent technologies, systems, and applications that connect models with knowledge, tools, agents, humans, and the physical world. Key topics span next-generation foundation and reasoning models, agentic and multi-agent intelligence, advanced memory, planning and tool use, model–agent interoperability, world and embodied intelligence, human–AI collaboration, and the safety, trustworthiness, and evaluation of emerging intelligent systems.

Beyond individual AI models and systems, Hyper-Intelligence (HyperI) investigates higher-level intelligent capabilities for accomplishing complex tasks through the hyper-connections, hyper-compositions, hyper-collaborations, and hyper-cognition of diverse intelligent entities. These entities may be human or artificial, physical or digital, and local or remote. HyperI provides an interdisciplinary perspective on how heterogeneous forms of intelligence can be integrated into hyper-intelligent systems capable of supporting complex problem solving, autonomous operation, scientific discovery, smart environments, and large-scale cyber-physical-social-human interaction. Key topics include hyper-connected and compositional intelligence, human–AI collaboration, collective and hybrid intelligence, cyber-physical-social-human systems, autonomous and adaptive systems, brain-inspired intelligence, trustworthy intelligence, and responsible HyperI.

This symposium aims to bring together researchers, practitioners, and experts from academia and industry to exchange recent advances, emerging ideas, and future perspectives on GAI and HyperI. It provides a forum for discussing their scientific foundations, enabling technologies, system architectures, practical applications, and societal implications. The symposium particularly welcomes interdisciplinary studies that explore how advanced AI and hyper-intelligent systems can enhance human capabilities, facilitate collaboration among humans and intelligent entities, and support the development of trustworthy, sustainable, and human-centered intelligent societies.

At least one of the authors of the accepted paper is requested to register and present the paper at the conference in hybrid mode (in person or virtually). All accepted papers are expected to be published in an IEEE Computer Society proceedings, subject to the final conference publication policy.

HiQ5 — Top 5 Questions for the Future of Hyper Intelligence

As a special brainstorming session of GAI-HyperI 2026, we are launching HiQ5 (Hyper-Intelligence Questions: Top 5)—an open, community-driven call to collect the most important open questions shaping the future of Generative AI and Hyper Intelligence. Everyone is invited to submit candidate questions; the organizers will curate and vote them down to a final Top 5, which will be reported and discussed in a dedicated panel session at the conference to spark lively, forward-looking exchanges and new collaborations.

Example questions to spark your thinking:
  • Connection & composition — How can heterogeneous intelligent entities—human or artificial, physical or digital, local or remote—be hyper-connected and composed into a single coherent, trustworthy hyper-intelligent system?
  • Emergence & collective intelligence — When and how does collective or hybrid intelligence emerge that exceeds the sum of its parts, and can we deliberately engineer that emergence?
  • Autonomy, adaptation & control — How can hyper-intelligent systems plan, act, and continually adapt over long horizons in the open cyber-physical-social world while staying safe, aligned, and under meaningful human control?
  • Measuring hyper-intelligence — How should we define and evaluate hyper-intelligence, with benchmarks that capture collaboration, cognition, and real-world impact beyond single-model accuracy?
  • Human-centered & responsible HyperI — How do we ensure hyper-intelligence augments rather than replaces human capability, and remains trustworthy, responsible, and beneficial across society?
